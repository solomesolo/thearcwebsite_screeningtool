Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to common questions about health screening and personalized testing

What screenings do I need at 40?

By age 40, most people benefit from a baseline set of preventive screenings. These often include: blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar (HbA1c), and certain cancer screenings depending on family history (e.g., mammogram, colon health). The exact tests depend on your personal risk factors, lifestyle, and medical history.

According to Guide to Clinical Preventive Services many of these screenings are recommended starting at age 40.

How do I know if I'm missing important health tests?

The best way is to map your personal history against established medical guidelines. Many people only do routine checkups but skip critical screenings like thyroid, vitamin deficiencies, or metabolic markers. A structured screening plan highlights the urgent, due, and optional tests so you can close any gaps.

What's the difference between a routine checkup and a screening plan?

A routine checkup is often broad, focusing on general health markers. A screening plan is personalized, based on age, family history, symptoms, and past test results. It pinpoints which tests are essential for you right now — so you don't miss hidden risks or waste money on irrelevant tests.

Why is evidence-based screening important?

Because not every test adds value. Evidence-based screening means your plan is based on medical research and global preventive guidelines, not trends or guesswork. This ensures your results are actionable and trustworthy.

Learn more from the Mayo Clinic about preventive health guidelines.

Can I do these screenings at home?

Yes, many common tests (cholesterol, HbA1c, vitamin D, hormones) can be done with finger-prick blood spot kits or saliva tests. Others (like imaging scans) require a clinic. A screening plan helps you know which can be done easily at home and which need a medical visit.
